# The Problem with Heritage

Nestlé is 150 years old. Their visual system carried that age — not as gravitas but as weight. Inconsistent logotypes across markets. A color palette that hadn't been interrogated since the 1990s. A typographic voice that whispered "institutional" when the brand needed to speak "human."

The brief was clear: modernize without erasure. The nest — the core symbol — had to survive. Everything else was negotiable.

## Research: Archaeology Before Architecture

The first two weeks were archival. I collected every major Nestlé visual touchpoint from 1905 to 2024: packaging, advertisements, annual reports, environmental signage, digital surfaces.

Patterns emerged. The nest symbol had quietly evolved across eight distinct versions. The wordmark had been redrawn eleven times. The color — Nestlé Red — had drifted from a deep crimson toward a saturated commodity red as digital printing became dominant.

> "Good brand work is archaeology. You dig before you build."

The brand had lost its depth. Not its recognition — that remained intact — but the sense that it had made intentional choices.

## System Redesign

The new visual system operates on three levels:

**Symbol.** The nest was redrawn from scratch at a 2:1 grid ratio. Strokes normalized. The bird removed — it had become a cliché. The nest alone carries more symbolic weight and scales better to 16px favicon use.

**Color.** Nestlé Red moved back toward its pre-digital heritage: `#B52025` — a red that reads as confident, not aggressive. Paired with an off-white (`#F7F2EB`) that carries warmth without nostalgia.

**Typography.** Primary: `GT America` — contemporary grotesque that references American commercial type history. Secondary: `Canela Text` for long-form editorial. Both chosen for their multilingual robustness across 189 markets.

## Rollout Considerations

A rebrand at this scale (189 countries, 2000+ products) has to account for:

- **Staged rollout** — new packaging only on reformulated products
- **Legal clearances** — trademark status of new logotype in all markets
- **Regional adaptations** — some markets required local script integration (Devanagari, Arabic)
- **Digital-first** — new system designed to the pixel before print

## Outcomes

This was a conceptual student project — real-world deployment wasn't the goal. The goal was to demonstrate that brand archaeology, systematic thinking, and rigorous visual craft could co-exist in a single design process.

The final system was presented to a panel of three practicing brand designers. Their consensus: the nest redrawn at 2:1 grid was the strongest formal decision in the project.
